# UX Psychology

Applying established psychological principles to improve usability and user experience.

## Key Laws of UX

### 1. Hick's Law
- **Principle**: The time it takes to make a decision increases with the number and complexity of choices.
- **Application**: 
  - Limit the number of options in menus and forms (max 5-7 per group)
  - Progressive disclosure: show advanced options only when needed
  - Chunk related options into categories
- **Anti-pattern**: Showing all form fields at once without grouping

### 2. Fitts's Law
- **Principle**: The time to acquire a target is a function of the distance to and size of the target.
- **Application**:
  - Make interactive elements (buttons, links) large enough to click easily (min 44x44px touch target)
  - Place important actions in easily reachable areas (avoid corners on mobile)
  - Use visual affordances (buttons should look clickable)
- **Anti-pattern**: Small, tightly packed links or buttons

### 3. Jakob's Law
- **Principle**: Users spend most of their time on other sites; they prefer your site to work the same way.
- **Application**:
  - Follow established design patterns and conventions
  - Don't reinvent common UI patterns (search in header, profile in corner)
  - Use familiar iconography (magnifying glass for search, gear for settings)
- **Anti-pattern**: Unique, unfamiliar interactions without clear onboarding

### 4. Miller's Law
- **Principle**: The average person can hold about 7 (plus or minus 2) items in working memory.
- **Application**:
  - Chunk information into groups of 5-7 elements
  - Use progress indicators for multi-step processes
  - Break complex forms into logical sections
- **Anti-pattern**: Showing 20+ items in a single list without grouping

### 5. Gestalt Principles
- **Proximity**: Related elements should be grouped together
- **Similarity**: Similar elements should share visual properties (color, shape, size)
- **Continuity**: Elements arranged in a line or curve are perceived as related
- **Closure**: Incomplete shapes are perceived as complete when recognizable
- **Common Region**: Elements within a bounded area are perceived as a group

## Application Checklist

- [ ] Are options limited and chunked appropriately?
- [ ] Are interactive elements large enough?
- [ ] Does the design follow familiar patterns?
- [ ] Is information grouped into 5-7 item chunks?
- [ ] Are related elements visually grouped?
